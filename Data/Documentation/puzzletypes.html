6. Puzzle Types

Documented here are the various types of puzzles supported by the program with a bit of discussion accompanying each one of them. The list proceeds in no particular order. Note that the names I gave to certain puzzle types did not always accurately reflect who invented them. Lastly, let me say that I'm not an internet scholar, so forgive me if I've missed some information here that a good researcher should have found.

6.1. Rubik3x3x3

This is the classic, well-known 3x3x3 cube invented by Erno Rubik in 1974. If you're new to twisty puzzling, this is a good place start.

6.2. Rubik2x2x2

This is a lower-dimensional equivilant of the 3x3x3, invented by Larry Nichols in 1970. This is an even easier place to start than the 3x3x3, and the knowledge gained here is directly transferrable to the 3x3x3 if you learn the key-hole method. Imagine a 3x3x3 where the edges and centers are invisible and what do you have? A 2x2x2.

6.3. Rubik2x2x3

I'm not sure who invented this one. Interestingly, it is easier than the 2x2x2, and it is certainly easier than the 2x3x3. Methods of solving this are transferable to the Square-1 as it can be found as a sub-group of the Square-1.

6.4. Rubik2x3x3

I believe this one too was invented by Erno Rubik. I've used move sequences for this puzzle on the 5x3x3 and the X-cube.

6.5. Skewb

According to Jaap, this one was invented by Tony Durham, but the name was coined by Douglas Hofstadter. Interestingly, only one algorithm is needed to solve this puzzle.

6.6. SkewbUltimate

This puzzle has exactly the same structure as the Skewb; it's just a different shape. It was produced by Uwe Meffert.

6.7. SquareOne

This one was invented by Karel Hrsel and Vojtech Kopsky, circa 1990. This is a great puzzle because of its ability to change shape. In my estimation, it is about as hard as the 3x3x3.
