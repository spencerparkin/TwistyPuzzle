2. Interfacing with the Puzzle

Upon program instantiation, you are immediately presented with a standard 3x3x3 Rubik's cube. It is not immediately obvious, because no cuts are visible on the cube, but if you click on the "Puzzle" menu, you'll see that the "Rubiks3x3x3" menu item is checked. You can use this menu to create other puzzle types.

In general, the often-combersome nature of manipulating a virtual twisty puzzle can make them much less fun to play with than their physical counter-parts. The intuitiveness and ease-of-use of the user-interface to such a puzzle, therefore, is of paramount importance; and that is why a number of ways of manipulating the puzzle have been provided as discussed in the following sub-sections. Dispite these difficulties, however, there are a number of obvious advantages to a computer simulation as I hope you'll see.

2.1. Orienting the Puzzle

Holding down the left mouse button with the cursor inside the window's drawing area while dragging the mouse, you'll see that the puzzle can be rotated so as to give you a view of the puzzle from almost any perspective. As of this writing, it is the only way of viewing the puzzle, but with practice, it is not hard to quickly orient the puzzle into the desired view. (I'm thinking of adding an X-box controller interface.)

2.2. Rotating Sections of the Puzzle

There are two ways to do this using the mouse, and a third way using the keyboard.

2.2.1. Rotate by Cut-Shape

By now you've probably noticed a few semi-transparent arrows poking out of the puzzle. These represent axes of rotation about which a cut-shape cuts-out and rotates a portion of the puzzle. After right-clicking on the arrow-tip of one of these axes, you'll see the cut-shape appear and arrow draw a bit brighter. You can then use the mouse wheel to rotate the portion of the puzzle intersected by the cut-shape.

Manually selecting the cut-shape axis like this can be annoying; so in that case, you may find it more convenient to turn on auto-selection of these axes. To do that, use the "Auto Select Axes" menu item under the "Interface" menu.

2.2.2. Rotate by Grip

This means of manipulating the puzzle was harder to implement and, consequently, a bit less reliable, but it is far more intuitive and less tedious than the previous method. As of this writing, it is best to generate all cuts of the puzzle before using this method, which is often the case after a good scrambling. Go to the "Program" menu and select "Scramble". After dismissing the subsequent dialog, wait for the puzzle to scramble, then move your mouse cursor over a face of the puzzle. Now, while holding down the right-mouse button, drag the mouse to see how the puzzle is manipulated. With practice, you can get a feel for how this works. Be sure to drag the mouse cursor in a straight line for best results.

2.2.3. Rotate by Sequence

Go to the "Render" menu and select "Draw Axis Labels." Axis labels should then appear next to each of the arrows poking out of the puzzle. There are two types of such labels: those that are assigned dynamically, and those that are statically assigned. If in the former case, the labels will changes as you orient the cube; otherwise, they always stay the same. For some puzzle, this dynamic labeling is convenient, for others, it's not.

Now click in the text-control at the bottom of the window, above the status bar. Here you can enter a command delimited sequence of rotation commands, each of which being simply the name of an axis of the puzzle with an "i" optionally appended.

2.2.4. Special Cases

Some puzzles have special moves that other puzzles do not. As of this writing, the Curvy Copter is the only example. To jumble an edge of the Curvy Copter, select its rotation axis, then rotate with the mouse wheel as usual, but while holding the ALT or ALT+SHIFT keys down.

2.3. Undo/Redo

If you make a mistake, you can always use the "Go Backward" menu option under the "History" menu. Note that as of this writing, it does not presist with the saving of your puzzle to disk.
