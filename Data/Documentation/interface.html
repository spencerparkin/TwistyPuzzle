2. Interfacing with the Puzzle

Upon program instantiation, you are immediately presented with a standard 3x3x3 Rubik's cube. It is not immediately obvious, because no cuts are visible on the cube, but if you click on the "Puzzle" menu, you'll see that the "Rubiks3x3x3" menu item is checked. You can use this menu to create other puzzle types.

The sub-sections that follow are worth reading, but if you'd like to watch a video introduction to the program, I tried to make one at "https://youtu.be/OVWdLHdGs2M". If this link fails, it may be that you need to install "snapd-xdg-open". Note that I failed to capture the mouse cursor in this video, but it should still be fairly instructive.

In general, the often-combersome nature of manipulating a virtual twisty puzzle can make them much less fun to play with than their physical counter-parts. The intuitiveness and ease-of-use of the user-interface to such a puzzle, therefore, is of paramount importance; and that is why a number of ways of manipulating the puzzle have been provided as discussed in the following sub-sections. Dispite these difficulties, however, there are a number of obvious advantages to a computer simulation as I hope you'll see.

2.1. Orienting the Puzzle

Holding down the left mouse button with the cursor inside the window's drawing area while dragging the mouse, you'll see that the puzzle can be rotated so as to give you a view of the puzzle from almost any perspective. While holding down the shift key, rotating the mouse wheel lets you zoom in and out.

2.2. Rotating Sections of the Puzzle

There are two ways to do this using the mouse, and a third way using the keyboard.

2.2.1. Rotate by Cut-Shape

By now you've probably noticed a few semi-transparent arrows poking out of the puzzle. These represent axes of rotation about which a cut-shape cuts-out and rotates a portion of the puzzle. After right-clicking on the arrow-tip of one of these axes, you'll see the cut-shape appear and the arrow draw a bit brighter. You can then use the mouse wheel to rotate the portion of the puzzle intersected by the selected cut-shape.

Manually selecting the cut-shape axis like this can be annoying; so in that case, you may find it more convenient to turn on auto-selection of these axes. To do that, use the "Auto Select Axes" menu item under the "Interface" menu. This causes the cut-shape axis nearest the mouse-ray to be selected at all times. Admittedly, this is an idea stolen from the well-known gelatin-brain program.

2.2.2. Rotate by Grip

This means of manipulating the puzzle was harder to implement and, consequently, a bit less reliable, but it is far more intuitive and less tedious than the previous method. As of this writing, it is best to generate all cuts of the puzzle before using this method, which is often the case after a good scrambling. Go to the "Program" menu and select "Scramble". After dismissing the subsequent dialog, wait for the puzzle to scramble, then move your mouse cursor over a face of the puzzle. Now, while holding down the right mouse button, drag the mouse to see how the puzzle is manipulated. With practice, you can get a feel for how this works. Be sure to drag the mouse cursor in a straight line for best results. If at all possible, click on a face of the puzzle that can only be affected by the desired rotation axis. I've noticed that with puzzle with many axes, it's just too hard to use this manipulation method. Furthermore, if a puzzle has overlapping, parallel rotation axes, I do not as yet have a way of determing which one is the intended axes of rotation.

2.2.3. Rotate by Sequence

Go to the "Render" menu and select "Draw Axis Labels" if it is not already checked. Axis labels should then appear next to each of the arrows poking out of the puzzle. There are two types of such labels: those that are assigned dynamically, and those that are statically assigned. If in the former case, the labels will changes as you orient the puzzle; otherwise, they always stay the same. For some puzzles, this dynamic labeling is convenient, for others, it isn't.

Now click in the text-control at the bottom of the window, above the status bar. Here you can enter a comma-delimited sequence of rotation commands, each of which being simply the name of an axis of the puzzle with an "i" optionally appended. For example, on the Rubik's Cube, you might enter "U,R,Ui,Li,U,Ri,Ui,L" without the quotes. Entering this sequence should enqueue 8 rotations as shown in the status bar. For puzzles with a special move, such as the CurveyCopter3, you can prepend an exclamation mark, or caret symbol to access the special move, and its alternate.

2.2.4. Special Cases

Some puzzles have special moves that other puzzles do not. As of this writing, the Curvy Copter and Curvy Copter 3 are the only examples. To jumble an edge of the Curvy Copter, select its rotation axis, then rotate with the mouse wheel as usual, but while holding the ALT or ALT+SHIFT keys down. The same goes for the Curvy Copter 3.

2.3. Undo/Redo

If you make a mistake, you can always use the "Go Backward" menu option under the "History" menu. Note that as of this writing, your rotation history does not presist with the saving of your puzzle to disk.

2.4. Face Colors

If you so desire, you can change the color of the faces of a puzzle by right-double-clicking on a face to bring up the color-choose dialog. Note that you can do this with any face, even after the puzzle has been scrambled. So if you want the keep the puzzle sane, choose your colors before you scramble the puzzle.

2.5. Working With Snapshots

There is an experimental feature designed to aide the user in his or her analysis of a move sequence. Working with a well-scrambled puzzle, choose the "Take Snap-shot" menu item from the "Interface" menu. Having taken a snap-shot of the puzzle, execute a rotation sequence in any way you'd like (e.g., use the command-line interface to quickly try a sequence you're working on.) Once the sequence is complete, choose the "Draw Diff" option from the "Render" menu to draw splines from a face's old location to its new location, if it moved at all.
