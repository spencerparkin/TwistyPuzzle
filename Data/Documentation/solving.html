3. Solving Twisty Puzzles

3.1 Coming up with your own solution

In a nutshell, coming up with your own solution amounts to finding enough useful sequences that can be used to work your way down through a subgroup chain to the trivial group. Of particular interest are sequences in the form of conjugates and commutators. Conjugates can often help you create new sequences from old ones. Commutators let you narrow or focus the net impact of a sequence based upon how much or little the two sequences taken in the commutator commute with one another.

3.2 The Program Solve

As of this writing, the program has a limited ability to solve some of the twisty puzzles. The solutions it finds are far from optimal, but they appear to work in all of my testing thus far. Note that you won't recognize any concrete solution method being used by the computer as it uses the sifting process on a pre-generated stabilizer-chain to come up with its factorizations. For those interested in the code, it can be found here: https://github.com/spencerparkin/PermutationGroup

3.3 Puzzle Notes

Here I'm going to keep a bunch of notes that I've collected over the years on solving various twisty puzzles. I encourage the reader to look at these notes only after they've come up with their own solution. It's much more satisfying to solve a puzzle having not resorted to any help, even if it takes a very long time, months even, to figure a puzzle out. In any case, I do not go into any great detail for most puzzles, perfering instead to document only the key concepts and sequences that are easy to forget, leaving the rest to the reader.
